Race Overview

The Mid South isn't just a gravel race‚Äîit's the event that kicks off America's gravel season each March. What started in 2013 as the Land Run 100 with just 121 riders has exploded into a multi-day dirt party drawing over 4,000 participants from across the globe. Founded by Bobby Wintle and Crystal Wintle of District Bicycles in Stillwater, Oklahoma, this race has become gravel cycling royalty.

The course winds through the iconic red clay roads and rolling hills of Oklahoma's Cross Timbers region. When it's dry, it's blazingly fast and tactical. When Mother Nature brings rain, those same roads transform into sticky, bike-clogging peanut butter that will test your will to live. Either way, you'll finish with stories to tell and Oklahoma dirt in places you didn't know existed.

"The Mid South doesn't need an introduction. It's a vibe. It's movement in community. It's the celebration that happens when thousands of people converge on a small college town to ride bikes, make friends, and party on red dirt." ‚Äî Bobby Wintle, Race Founder

The Course

100-Mile Route

The course changes slightly year to year based on road conditions and Bobby's vision, but typically runs 100-107 miles with 3,000-4,000 feet of elevation gain. Don't let the modest climbing fool you‚Äîthe rolling terrain is relentless, and the technical sections will humble you.

Key Features

  • Creek crossings requiring dismount
  • Steep climbs immediately after crossings
  • Singletrack section around mile 90
  • Technical descents on loose gravel
  • Sand traps that stop you dead
  • Rutted red clay (dry or muddy)
  • Open country riding with exposure to wind

Critical Sections

  • Mile 13: Creek crossing - positioning crucial
  • Mile 25: Marshy two-track (CX skills needed)
  • Mile 52: Dry creek crossing + steep climb
  • Mile 82: Loose roadbed section
  • Mile 90: Singletrack (ride or hike?)
  • Final 10 miles: Exposed time trial to finish

50-Mile Route

  • ~56 miles actual distance
  • ~2,000 feet of elevation
  • Same terrain challenges, shorter distance
  • Perfect for first-timers
  • Still a serious workout
  • Same legendary finish line experience

Terrain & Conditions

Oklahoma's red clay is simultaneously beautiful and brutal. In dry years (2022-2024), the hard-packed dirt creates fast, smooth racing with average speeds over 20mph for the pros. When it rains, those same roads become a sticky, bike-destroying nightmare where you'll carry more mud weight than bike weight.

The "Cross Timbers" region features rolling terrain with countless small climbs‚Äîthe elevation profile looks like shark teeth. You're never truly on flat ground, which means constant power variance and tactical positioning in groups. Add in the notorious Oklahoma wind (often from the south), and pack dynamics become critical.

This race will expose your weaknesses. Think you're fit? The constant rollers and tactical racing will test that fitness differently than steady-state efforts. Think you can handle your bike? You need to know how to ride in a pack on variable terrain. And you need to be ready for literally anything‚Äîbecause Oklahoma weather can turn a fast, dry race into an epic mud battle in 24 hours.

Race Day Strategy

Start & Early Miles

In 2025, the race added pro wave starts 15 minutes before the mass start at 8am. If you're in the amateur field, positioning in the first 500 riders is critical‚Äîthat creek crossing at mile 13 becomes a bottleneck, and being mid-pack means waiting, losing momentum, and burning matches to chase back.

The pace goes out fast. Really fast. Pros average over 23mph in dry conditions. Don't get sucked into chasing the leaders unless you're prepared to redline for 100 miles. Find a group matching your fitness and settle in for the tactical game.

Mid-Race Tactics

The race typically splinters after the mile 52 creek crossing and subsequent climb. This is where strong riders attack to shed weaker climbers. If you're hanging on by fingernails, let it go‚Äîthere's still 50 miles to race, and burning all your matches here leaves nothing for the finish.

Aid stations are crucial. Practice your feed zone skills beforehand‚Äîgrabbing bottles while riding, quick stops without losing too much time. The group you're with likely won't wait if you faff around for 5 minutes eating a sandwich.

The Finish

That late singletrack section around mile 90 is the final selection point. Exit it in a strong group, and you've got a chance at a good finish. Exit it alone or in a weak group, and you're time trialing to the line. The final 10 miles are often into a headwind‚Äîthis is where mental toughness trumps fitness.

Equipment Recommendations

Bike & Tires

Bike: Any gravel bike will work, but bikes with better clearance handle the mud/ruts better. Drop bars are standard. Consider geometry that's more aggressive if racing, more comfortable if surviving.

Tires: This is the million-dollar question. In dry years (most recent), 38-42mm semi-slicks or full slicks are fastest. Lauren De Crescenzo won 2024 on 40mm Maxxis Velocita slicks. If rain threatens, you need tread‚Äîbut too aggressive and you'll be slow on the fast sections. Volume is your friend for comfort and traction. Tubeless is mandatory.

Tire Pressure: 30-40psi depending on weight, tire volume, and conditions. Lower in mud for traction, higher in dry for speed. Use a calculator (Silca) as a starting point, then adjust based on recon rides.

Gearing

Standard gravel gearing (40T chainring, 10-44T cassette or similar) works well. The climbs aren't crushing, but you'll want that bailout gear for steep pitches after creek crossings when you're fatigued. If you're strong and racing, a 1x setup with 42-44T front and 10-36T rear gives you speed on fast sections.

What to Carry

  • 2-3 bottles (aid stations are spaced 30-40 miles)
  • Spare tube + inflator (even if tubeless)
  • Multi-tool and chain tool
  • Tire boot and extra valve core
  • Enough nutrition for 5-6 hours
  • Light jacket if weather is questionable
  • GPS computer with course loaded

Clothing

Training Plan Essentials

Volume & Intensity

To race the 100-miler competitively, you need to be holding 18-25 hours per week in the final build block. To finish respectably, 12-15 hours works. The 50-miler can be done on 8-12 hours per week of consistent training.

The key is specificity: this is NOT an all-day endurance grind. It's 4-6 hours of variable intensity with surges, threshold efforts, and tactical racing. Your training needs to reflect that.

Key Workout Types

Priority Workouts

  • Sweet Spot Intervals: 3-4 x 15-20min at 88-93% FTP. Builds the aerobic base to hold tempo in groups.
  • Over/Unders: Alternating between threshold and slightly above (95-105% FTP). Simulates pack surges and attacks.
  • VO2 Max Efforts: 5-8 x 3-5min hard efforts. Develops the punch to stay with attacks and crest climbs with the group.
  • Long Group Rides: 3-5 hours of variable intensity with others. Practice pack skills, feeding on the bike, and mental toughness.
  • CX/MTB Skills: Practice remounting/dismounting, riding technical terrain, handling mud if possible.

Periodization

Lauren De Crescenzo's 2024 preparation (coached by Frank Overton) offers a blueprint: high volume base building through December-January (20-25 hrs/wk), then a 3-week sharpening phase with specific intensity, followed by a mini-taper. She did a practice race (BorderWars) 3 weeks out at similar duration/intensity to test fitness and strategy.

Gravel-Specific Prep

Don't just train watts‚Äîtrain skills. Find rough roads or trails to practice bike handling. Do remount/dismount drills until they're automatic. Practice eating and drinking while riding on bumpy terrain. If you can find red clay or sticky mud, ride in it to understand how it affects your bike.

The Weekend Experience

Thursday: Beer Release Party

The weekend kicks off Thursday evening at Iron Monk Brewery with a custom Mid South beer release. This is where you meet riders from around the world, get stoked on the vibe, and realize this is more than just a bike race.

Friday: Running Races & Shakeout Rides

The 50K ultramarathon and half marathon run on Friday morning. Meanwhile, cyclists can join organized shakeout rides to preview sections of the course and dial in equipment. The expo opens with vendors, swag, and the energy building toward Saturday.

Saturday: Race Day

Pro waves start around 7:45am, mass start at 8am. Finishers roll in from roughly noon until late afternoon. Bobby Wintle stands at the finish line to hug every. Single. Rider. This tradition‚Äînow 13 years strong‚Äîis as much a part of The Mid South as the red clay itself.

Post-race, the celebration continues with live music, food, and the kind of camaraderie that only comes from shared suffering and triumph. The DFL (dead f*cking last) gets as much celebration as the winner.

The Double

For the truly ambitious (or insane), "The Mid South Double" means running the 50K on Friday, then riding the 100-mile gravel race on Saturday. In 2024, nearly 200 people attempted this combo. It's exactly as brutal as it sounds and earns you forever glory in Oklahoma gravel lore.

Logistics & Planning

Registration

Registration opens in September (typically late September). The 100-mile spots fill within hours for the pro wave, and within a few days for the mass start. The 50-mile takes longer but still sells out. Set an alarm, have your credit card ready, and don't hesitate.

Lodging

Stillwater is a college town (Oklahoma State University) with decent hotel options, but 4,000 visitors overwhelm capacity. Book immediately after registering. Options include hotels, Airbnbs, camping (the expo allows overnight parking/car camping), or the university area.

Getting There

Stillwater is 70 miles northeast of Oklahoma City (Will Rogers World Airport - OKC) and 70 miles west of Tulsa (Tulsa International Airport - TUL). Both airports work, with OKC typically cheaper but TUL slightly closer. Rental cars book up, so reserve early.

Course Recon

The course is published a few weeks before the race. If you're serious about racing, arrive early and ride key sections. The creek crossings, technical bits, and finish need to be previewed. Local riders often organize recon rides‚Äîjoin the Mid South Facebook group for details.

üî• 2025 Wildfire Cancellation: Lessons Learned

In March 2025, The Mid South made the difficult but correct decision to cancel the race due to catastrophic wildfires and hurricane-force winds that swept across Oklahoma. Over 130 separate fires burned across 44 counties, emergency services were overwhelmed, and evacuations were underway in Stillwater itself.

Bobby Wintle's transparency and community-first decision making was exemplary. Rather than prioritize the event, they prioritized safety‚Äîboth for participants and for the strained emergency services. The community response was equally impressive, with riders pivoting from race mode to fundraising mode, raising money for wildfire relief and supporting affected families.

Takeaway: Mother Nature is unpredictable, and sometimes the race you trained for doesn't happen. The mature response is to support the community, be flexible, and remember that this sport is about more than results. The Mid South's handling of this crisis only reinforced why this event is so beloved.
