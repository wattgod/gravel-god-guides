Training Zones

These zones are based on your FTP. If you don't know your FTP, do a 20-minute test and multiply by 0.95.

Zone Name % FTP RPE Description
Z1 Active Recovery <55% 1-2 Easy spinning. Conversation flows effortlessly. Use for recovery rides and warm-ups.
Z2 Endurance 55-75% 3-4 All-day pace. Can speak in full sentences. This is where aerobic base is built.
Z3 Tempo 76-87% 5-6 Comfortably hard. Conversation limited to short phrases. Sustainable for 1-3 hours.
G G Spot 88-92% 6-7 The money zone for gravel. Hard but sustainable. Where races are won.
Z4 Threshold 93-105% 7-8 Race pace for shorter events. Sustainable for 20-60 minutes max.
Z5 VO2max 106-120% 9 Hard breathing. 3-8 minute intervals. Builds top-end power.
Z6 Anaerobic >120% 10 All-out efforts. 30 seconds to 2 minutes. Sprints and attacks.
The G Spot

The "G Spot" (88-92% FTP) is our signature zone for gravel racing. Hard enough to be race-relevant, sustainable enough for long efforts. This is where gravel races are won.

TrainingPeaks Glossary

These are the metrics and terms you'll encounter in TrainingPeaks.

FTP
Functional Threshold Power. The highest power you can sustain for approximately one hour. All training zones are based on this number.
TSS
Training Stress Score. A measure of workout load based on duration and intensity. 1 hour at FTP = 100 TSS.
CTL
Chronic Training Load. 42-day rolling average of daily TSS. Proxy for 'fitness.' Good amateurs: 60-80. Pros: 140+.
ATL
Acute Training Load. 7-day rolling average of daily TSS. Proxy for 'fatigue.'
TSB
Training Stress Balance. Yesterday's CTL minus yesterday's ATL. Proxy for 'form' or freshness. Negative = fatigued, positive = fresh. Race best at +5 to +20.
NP
Normalized Power. Estimate of the constant power that would produce the same physiological cost as your variable power output.
IF
Intensity Factor. Ratio of Normalized Power to FTP (IF = NP ÷ FTP). IF of 1.0 means you averaged FTP.
W/kg
Watts per kilogram. Power relative to body weight. Good amateurs ~4 W/kg, elite pros 6+ W/kg.
VI
Variability Index. NP divided by Average Power. Shows how variable your power was. 1.0 = perfectly steady.
EF
Efficiency Factor. NP divided by Average HR. Higher = better aerobic efficiency. Track over time.
Pw:Hr
Aerobic Decoupling. Compares first-half to second-half power-to-HR ratio. <5% = good aerobic fitness.
PMC
Performance Management Chart. Graph showing CTL, ATL, and TSB over time. The main planning tool in TrainingPeaks.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion. Subjective 1-10 scale of how hard an effort feels. Essential when power isn't available.
Z2
Zone 2, Endurance zone. 55-75% FTP. The foundation of aerobic training.
FatMax
Fat oxidation maximum. The intensity where your body burns the most fat per minute. Usually upper Z2.
VO2max
Maximum oxygen uptake. Your aerobic ceiling. Improved with high-intensity intervals.
Anaerobic Capacity
Your ability to produce power above threshold. Like a matches—burn them wisely.
HRV
Heart Rate Variability. Variation in time between heartbeats. Higher generally = better recovered.
Periodization
Systematic variation of training load over time. Prevents plateaus and manages fatigue.
Polarized Training
80% easy (Z1-Z2) / 20% hard (Z4+). Minimal time in the middle. Research-backed approach.
Block Periodization
Concentrated training blocks focusing on one quality at a time.
Traditional Pyramidal
Most volume at low intensity, progressively less at higher intensities. Classic endurance approach.
Taper
Planned reduction in training load before a race. Maintains intensity, drops volume.
Openers
Short, sharp efforts 1-2 days before race. Activates systems without creating fatigue.

Gravel Skills

These skills separate good gravel racers from everyone else.

Loose Surface Cornering

Lean the bike, not your body. Weight the outside pedal. Look through the turn, not at the ground. Enter wide, apex late, exit wide. Scrub speed before the turn, not during.

Cadence Control

Slow twitch fibers (<80 rpm) for maintaining speed on flats. Fast twitch fibers (>95 rpm) for changing speed and steep hills. Practice varying cadence to develop feel for what works when.

Braking Technique

Feather brakes gradually—never grab. Front brake does most of the stopping. Rear brake for fine adjustments. Brake before turns, not during. Practice controlled rear-wheel skidding on low-risk gravel to learn limits.

Steep Climbs (15-25%)

Keep momentum going in—you won't get it back. Stay seated for rear wheel traction. Weight over rear wheel, hands light on bars. Standing shifts weight forward and loses traction. Lower gear than you think you need.

Track Standing

Develops balance and handling ability. Start on slight uphill, front wheel turned slightly uphill. Gentle pedal pressure forward, handlebar corrections for balance. Practice until you can hold 30+ seconds.

Bunny Hopping

For obstacles you can't avoid. Compress down → pull up on bars → scoop back with feet → level out in air. Start with small obstacles. Even a 2-inch hop saves your wheels from impacts.

Eating While Riding

Practice unwrapping bars, opening bottles, and eating at pace. Find what foods you can eat at threshold. Never try new nutrition on race day.

Group Riding & Pace Lines

Hold your line. No sudden movements. Point out hazards. Pull through smoothly, peel off into the wind. Take shorter pulls into headwinds. Communication is everything.

Mechanical Self-Sufficiency

Practice fixing flats until it's automatic (target: under 5 minutes). Know how to adjust brakes and derailleurs. Carry the right tools. The race ends when you can't fix it.

Practice Recommendations

Take your gravel bike on MTB trails (green/blue difficulty). Set up obstacle courses in grassy fields. 30 minutes of focused practice builds skills faster than 3 hours of just riding.

Race Day Tactics

It turns out racing is much more pleasant if you employ something called "tactics." Tactics just mean having a plan for how you're going to use your energy over the course.

The Three-Act Structure

Every long gravel race follows a predictable three-act structure.

Phase When What's Happening Your Job
THE MADNESS 0-90 min Fresh legs, nervous energy, crowded course. Attacks fly. Adrenaline-fueled decisions. Go hard enough to find your group. Don't panic. Start eating immediately.
FALSE DAWN 90 min - 3 hrs Order returns. Groups stabilize. Pace becomes predictable. Feels manageable. Stay fueled and hydrated. Contribute to your group without overworking. Bank energy.
THE PIPER 3+ hrs to finish The bill comes due. Under-fueled riders bonk. Under-prepared riders cramp. Mentally weak riders check out. Execute your plan while others fall apart. This is where the race happens.

Tactical Principles

Be Efficient

Keep asking yourself this question:

The Key Question

"How could I keep going at this speed for less effort?"

The goal of racing, unlike training, is not putting up big power numbers—it's to go from A to B as fast as possible using the least amount of energy.

Draft Smart

Even on gravel, drafting helps. Don't be a hero. Take pulls, but don't try to show anyone how strong you are. Every minute at the front is energy you're not saving. Every minute in the draft is energy you're banking for later.

Read the Course

Always pick the best possible line. If the right line is packed down and smooth, why are you riding in potholes? Look ahead. Pick lines. Save watts.

Read the Wind

Headwind? Worth putting out more energy to put time on people. Tailwind? Your speed will be higher but you won't gain as much time for proportional power output. Don't hammer with a tailwind—save that for when it turns into a slog.
