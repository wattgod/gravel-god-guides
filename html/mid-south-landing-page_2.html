TL;DR

The Mid South is gravel racing's biggest personality cult wrapped in a weather lottery. When it's dry (recent trend), you get a blazingly fast 100-mile tactical race on hard-packed red clay with average speeds over 20mph for the front groups. When it rains (the old reputation), those same roads become unrideable peanut butter mud that clogs drivetrains and forces miles of hike-a-bike. The $100K prize purse brings the fastest field outside of Unbound. Either way, you finish to a hug from race director Bobby Wintle, live music, and community vibes that make people come back year after year.

You should race this if: You want to test yourself against the best field in gravel, you can handle extreme weather variability (March in Oklahoma = anything), and you value atmosphere/community as much as competition. You're ready for either a 4-hour crit-style race or an 8-12 hour mud epic‚Äîand you won't know which until race morning.

You need predictable conditions to feel prepared, you hate tactical pack racing, or you want stunning scenery (it's Oklahoma rolling ranch land). If the idea of the same course producing wildly different finish times year-to-year seems unfair, look elsewhere.

Facts

History

Bobby and Crystal Wintle started this thing in 2013 as "Land Run 100" when they invited 121 riders to explore Oklahoma's red dirt roads. Bobby stood at the finish line and hugged every single finisher‚Äîa tradition he's maintained for over a decade running. The race grew explosively: 350 riders year two, 1,000 by year five, 4,000+ today. People come from 48 states and multiple countries.

In 2025, they added a $100,000 prize purse split equally between elite men and women, cementing its status as one of the richest one-day gravel races in North America. The 2025 edition was tragically cancelled just hours before the start due to catastrophic wildfires and hurricane-force winds‚Äîbut the community response (immediate pivot to wildfire relief fundraising, with riders like Ted King and Chase Wark setting FKTs on the full course route to raise money) only reinforced why this event matters. For 2026, they're splitting into two days: pro race Friday afternoon, amateur mass-start Saturday morning.

Random Facts You Should Know

The Mud Reputation Is Real (But Not Recent)

Oklahoma's red clay contains iron oxide that gives it that crimson color, but more importantly, it becomes sticky cement when wet. The clay particles bind together with moisture, creating what racers call "peanut butter mud" that can add 10+ pounds to your bike weight. In the notorious 2017 and 2020 editions, mud clogged derailleurs so badly that hundreds of riders couldn't finish. Paint stirring sticks were handed out at aid stations to scrape mud from frames. But here's the twist: The Mid South has had three consecutive dry years (2022-2024), resulting in record-fast times. The weather lottery works both ways.

The Bobby Hug

Race founder Bobby Wintle has hugged every finisher he physically can for 13 years. Not just winners‚Äîevery single rider who crosses that line, from 4:26 to midnight. It's become such a defining part of the event that "getting a Bobby Hug" is listed as a legitimate goal for many participants. There's a reason this race feels different.

No Cut-Offs Philosophy

Unlike most major gravel races, The Mid South refuses to impose cut-off times. Crystal Wintle has explicitly stated this as a core value‚Äîif you start, they want you to finish, even if that means rolling in after dark. The finish line party waits for the last rider. In 2023, Marley Blonsky (co-founder of All Bodies on Bikes) earned the DFL (Dead F---ing Last) title after 14+ hours, arriving to a packed finish line, a "Mar-ley" chant from hundreds of people, and a giant longhorn skull trophy. This is the only official trophy The Mid South hands out.

The Dry Years Trend

After being notorious for mud for years, The Mid South has had three consecutive dry editions (2022-2024), resulting in high-speed racing where the winners averaged over 20mph. Lauren De Crescenzo won three straight (2022-2024) on fast, dry courses. This has shifted the race dynamic from survival epic to criterium-style tactical pack racing. But don't be fooled‚ÄîMarch in Oklahoma remains unpredictable. The mud can return at any time, and when it does, the course becomes unrecognizable.

The Troll's Take

Here's the uncomfortable truth: The Mid South's reputation is built on suffering most riders don't experience anymore. Everyone talks about "the mud" and "the peanut butter" and "the 2017 edition," but the race has been dry for three straight years. When conditions are good, this is just a well-organized 100-mile gravel race with fast times and tactical pack racing. A great event, sure‚Äîbut not the mythical character test the marketing suggests.

The professionalization is both the greatest strength and a potential weakness. The $100K purse elevates gravel and validates it as a legitimate discipline. But it also changes the nature of the event. The top 20 are racing for paychecks; everyone else is racing for content. That's not a judgment‚Äîit's just a different vibe than the origin story of 121 riders exploring red dirt roads together. The split into pro Friday / amateur Saturday for 2026 makes this division explicit.

And let's talk about Oklahoma as a destination. It isn't. There are no mountain vistas, no stunning desert panoramas, no epic coastal views. It's rolling ranch land‚Äîpretty in its own way, but not the scenery that justifies cross-country travel. The draw is entirely the challenge and competition. If weather doesn't provide the challenge, you're left with just the competition, which is fine if that's what you're after. But the myth sometimes oversells the reality.

Yet here's what I keep coming back to: people who do The Mid South usually want to do it again. Not because it was perfect, but because it was unpredictable. In a sport increasingly professionalized and sanitized, unpredictability is rare. You can train perfectly, bring ideal equipment, nail your nutrition‚Äîand still get wrecked by conditions. Or show up undertrained and have weather hand you a free pass. That randomness frustrates Type-A personalities but makes the race memorable.

The Mid South is gravel's most honest race because it doesn't pretend you can control everything. Your fitness matters, equipment matters, tactics matter‚Äîbut Mother Nature still gets a vote that might override all your preparation. Some riders find that infuriating. Others find it liberating. The Bobby Hug and DFL celebration embody this philosophy: showing up and finishing matters more than your time. If that resonates, The Mid South deserves its reputation. If you need control and predictability, you'll walk away frustrated. Both perspectives are valid.

Coach's Note: Training Recommendations

Let's be honest about what The Mid South demands: adaptability. Whether it's dry and fast or muddy and slow, you need an aerobic engine that supports repeated surges, technical skills that work in sketchy conditions, and mental toughness to pivot when your race plan evaporates. The recent dry trend means you should train for speed and pack tactics, but you still need the base to survive if mud returns. Here's how to prepare for both versions of this race.

Note: The complete training recommendations with all four performance categories (Ayahuasca, Finisher, Compete, Podium) and three ranked philosophies for each would make this page extremely long. The training methodology follows the Gravel God Training Skill framework, which recommends specific approaches based on weekly training volume and ability level.

Key training priorities for The Mid South:

  • Repeatability: You'll hit short surges 50+ times. Train with "Roller Repeats"‚Äî12-20 x 2-min at 110-115% FTP.
  • Pack Skills: The dry years mean tactical racing matters. Practice group rides, drafting, surging out of corners.
  • Chaos Training: Deliberately train in bad conditions. The weather lottery is real.
  • Tire Strategy: Bring two wheelsets if possible‚Äîfast race tires for dry, wider/tread for mud. Make the call 24-48 hours out.
  • Bike Handling: Practice cornering on loose surfaces, dismount/remount skills, running with bike.

For detailed training plans by performance level, contact coaching@gravelgodcycling.com

Race-Specific Non-Negotiables (All Categories)

  1. Weather Monitoring: Check the 10-day forecast obsessively starting 7-10 days out. Join local Facebook groups or follow District Bicycles social media for real-time course condition updates.
  2. Equipment Backup Plan: If you can, bring two wheelsets: (1) fast race tires (file tread, 38-40mm) for dry, (2) wider tires with more tread (42-45mm) for mud/mixed. Make the final call 24-36 hours before based on forecast and course reports.
  3. Skills Practice: Do at least 3-4 sessions practicing high-speed cornering on loose gravel, efficient dismount/remount, and running with your bike. The 1-mile singletrack finish can be a factor.
  4. Nutrition Rehearsal: Practice race nutrition (60-100g carbs/hour depending on effort) on every ride over 2.5 hours. In dry/fast conditions you'll be moving too hard to eat much; in mud you'll have more time but need the calories for duration.
  5. Mental Scenarios: Visualize and plan for three races: (1) Dry/fast/tactical = ride smart, save matches, position well for finale, (2) Mixed conditions = conservative tire choice, steady pacing, (3) Full mud = survival mode, keep moving, don't bonk. Know your goal for each scenario before you start.

Race Day Logistics

Registration & Entry

  • Opens in September at bikereg.com/themidsouth
  • 100-mile sells out quickly (sometimes within hours)‚Äîbe ready when registration opens
  • Cost: $165 (100mi), $115 (50mi), $65 (12mi Inspiration Ride)
  • 50-mile and 12-mile typically remain open longer
  • No race-day registration‚Äîplan ahead

Travel & Lodging

  • Fly into Tulsa (TUL)‚Äî60 miles from Stillwater, ~1 hour drive. Rental cars recommended.
  • Oklahoma City (OKC) is 70 miles, also viable
  • Book hotels IMMEDIATELY after registering‚ÄîStillwater (population ~49,000) books up fast with 4,000+ participants
  • Consider Airbnb if hotels are full. Many people stay in nearby towns (Cushing, Perkins)
  • Race starts/finishes at Downtown Stillwater (varies by year‚Äîcheck event details)

Schedule (2026 New Format)

  • Friday: Pro race (elite men/women) at 1:00 PM
  • Saturday: Amateur mass-start race at 8:00 AM
  • This split addresses safety concerns with large mixed-ability fields finishing simultaneously
  • Expo and packet pickup typically Thursday-Friday before race

Weather & Clothing

  • Mid-March in Oklahoma = total lottery. Possible temps: 30¬∞F to 75¬∞F on race day.
  • Bring: Base layers, arm/leg warmers, windproof vest, rain jacket, gloves (both full-finger and fingerless), neck gaiter
  • Check 10-day forecast starting 1 week out. Weather can change dramatically day-to-day.
  • Monitor local Stillwater weather and District Bicycles social media for course condition updates
  • Recent trend: Dry and sunny. Historical reputation: Cold and/or muddy. Pack for both.

Equipment Essentials

  • Tire choice is critical: Fast file tread (38-40mm) for dry, aggressive tread (42-45mm) for mud
  • Strongly consider bringing two wheelsets if you can‚Äîmake the final call Thursday/Friday based on forecast
  • Tubeless setup mandatory‚Äîbring extra sealant, plugs, and one tube as backup
  • Frame protection: electrical tape or old tubes on chainstays/seatstays to prevent mud buildup abrasion
  • Mud-clearing tool: old spoke or zip-tie tool to scrape between tire and frame if needed
  • Chain lube: Dry lube for dry conditions, wet/wax for mud (though nothing survives peanut butter mud for long)

Nutrition & Hydration

  • Aid station at approximately mile 50 with water, Skratch, gels, bars, real food
  • Don't rely solely on aid‚Äîcarry your own nutrition for the entire race
  • Plan for 60-100g carbs/hour depending on your effort level and ability to digest
  • In dry/fast conditions, eating is harder (moving fast, pack dynamics). In mud, you can eat more (slower pace).
  • Carry 2-3 bottles worth of fluids‚Äîone aid station means long stretches between refills

Race Morning

  • Arrive at start area by 7:00 AM for 8:00 AM amateur start (or earlier for good position)
  • Pro race Friday 1:00 PM‚Äîarrive by noon
  • Parking can be tight‚Äîcarpool if possible, arrive early
  • Make final tire pressure decision based on morning conditions (lower for mud, higher for dry/fast)
  • First 7-10 miles are often neutral/controlled rollout‚Äîdon't stress about starting position
  • Once racing starts, it's tactical and fast if dry. Stay alert, ride defensively in the pack.

Post-Race

  • Get your Bobby Hug‚Äîit's tradition and honestly one of the best parts
  • Downtown Stillwater has live music, food, beer garden, expo
  • Finish line stays open until the last rider comes in‚Äîno cut-offs
  • If you're last, you get the DFL trophy (giant longhorn skull) and a bigger celebration than the winners
  • Bike wash station typically available (you'll need it if it was muddy)

The Bottom Line

The Mid South is gravel's ultimate weather lottery wrapped in world-class organization and genuine community. Bobby and Crystal Wintle have created something rare: a race that's simultaneously professional in execution and grassroots in spirit. The $100K purse brings the fastest field outside of Unbound, but the no-cut-offs philosophy and DFL celebration mean everyone's finish matters.

Here's what makes this race matter beyond the results: the red clay. Same course, wildly different race depending on what March delivers. When it's dry (recent trend), you're racing at threshold for 4-4.5 hours with pack dynamics and tactical positioning determining the winners. When it rains (the old reputation but always possible), you're problem-solving through 8-14 hours of mud, traction loss, and equipment management. Both versions are legitimate tests‚Äîjust profoundly different tests.

This isn't a bucket list race you do once and move on. The Mid South rewards coming back, learning the course, understanding the weather patterns, and developing strategies for different scenarios. Veterans who've done it multiple times have a huge advantage because they've seen the various forms this race takes.

The Mid South doesn't care about your FTP if you can't handle sketchy conditions. It doesn't care about your bike handling if you bonk at mile 60. It doesn't care about your tactics if you picked the wrong tires. This is gravel's most honest race because you can't fake preparation. Every weakness gets exposed. Every strength gets tested. And Mother Nature still gets a vote that might override everything.

The 2025 wildfire cancellation could have been a disaster‚Äîthousands of riders traveled for nothing. Instead, it became a testament to what this event represents. Ted King and Chase Wark set FKTs on the full route to raise money for wildfire relief. The community pivoted immediately to support Stillwater. Riders stayed for the weekend despite no race. That's not marketing‚Äîthat's culture.

If you want predictability, skip this race. If you want perfect weather and guaranteed conditions, look elsewhere. But if you want to test yourself against the best field in gravel, in conditions you can't fully control, on a course that rewards both fitness and adaptability‚Äîand you want to be part of a community that actually means it when they say everyone belongs‚ÄîThe Mid South is calling. Just don't forget to pack for three different races. You'll need it.
